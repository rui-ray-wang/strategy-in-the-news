📖 Instructor Example

CNBC · January 12, 2026

Apple picks Google’s Gemini to run AI-powered Siri coming this year

After Apple's internally-built Siri AI failed quality tests (reportedly "failing up to a third of the time"), Apple chose Google's Gemini model to power a completely new version of Siri. Google already pays Apple roughly $20 billion a year to remain the default search engine on iPhone — Apple now turns to the same company to supply the intelligence layer of its most personal interface.

Five Forces RBV Differentiation
I What caught your attention?
Google pays Apple roughly $20 billion a year for the right to be the default search engine on every iPhone. That arrangement made Apple one of Google's most important distribution partners — and gave Apple a revenue stream that required no engineering. Now Apple is turning to the same company to supply the intelligence layer of a rebuilt Siri. The direction of dependency has shifted: Apple used to collect from Google; now it is paying Google for a capability it could not build itself. Apple has spent thirty years building a reputation on owning the experience end to end — the chip, the OS, the app store, the store where you buy the device. Here, they couldn't. And they said so publicly. Craig Federighi told reporters Siri was "failing up to a third of the time" in internal tests. That kind of admission is rare. It tells you the internal pressure to ship something competitive was greater than the instinct to protect the image.
II Framework analysis
Five Forces — Supplier Power: Apple and Google have long had a mutually dependent relationship. Google needed iPhone distribution; Apple needed search revenue. The bargaining looked roughly balanced. This deal changes that. Apple now relies on Google for search and for the core intelligence layer of Siri. Google has become something closer to essential infrastructure for the iPhone experience. When a supplier becomes infrastructure, the buyer's exit options shrink — and pricing power in future negotiations shifts accordingly.

RBV: Apple's chip design, its OS integration, its manufacturing relationships — these remain genuinely hard to replicate. What this story reveals is that the model layer requires a fundamentally different organizational capability: massive datasets, rapid iteration on open-ended language tasks, comfort with probabilistic output. Apple's culture — perfectionism, secrecy, tight release cycles — may be precisely what made building a frontier LLM difficult. A company's deepest strengths can also define the boundary of what it can build.
The analytical move here is to ask not just which framework applies, but what the framework lets you see that you couldn't see before. Five Forces shows you a power shift. RBV shows you a capability boundary. Together they raise a more interesting question than either does alone.
III A question the article left me thinking about
Apple's differentiation has never been about having the fastest processor or the most features. It's been about the experience feeling seamless, premium, and trustworthy. The bet here is that privacy becomes the differentiator in AI. Apple's system processes queries through what it calls Private Cloud Compute — requests leave the device but are never stored or tied to a user identity. Google, Microsoft, and Meta cannot make that claim credibly, because their revenue models depend on knowing who you are.

The bet gets tested at a specific moment: when a Siri response is wrong, slow, or embarrassing, and the user doesn't know or care that Gemini is underneath it. Apple owns the brand; Google owns the model. If the AI experience disappoints, Apple absorbs the reputational cost. That's the structural risk in any differentiation strategy built on a licensed capability — you're staking your position on something you don't fully control.
A question the article left me thinking about: Apple's differentiation has historically required controlling every layer of the experience. If the intelligence layer is now Google's, what exactly is Apple differentiating on — and is that enough to justify the premium over Android?
